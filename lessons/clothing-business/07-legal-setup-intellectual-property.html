Legal Setup & Intellectual Property

Protect Yourself and Your Designs

Open interactive version (quiz + challenge)

Real-world analogy

Setting up the legal side of your clothing business is like putting on a seatbelt before driving. It's not exciting, it's not why you got in the car, and you hope you never need it. But the one time something goes wrong — a customer lawsuit, a brand stealing your name, or a tax audit — you'll be incredibly grateful you buckled up.

What is it?

Legal setup and intellectual property protection covers the legal foundations every clothing brand needs: business structure, registration, trademarks, copyrights, labeling compliance, insurance, permits, and contracts. While not glamorous, getting these right protects you from lawsuits, prevents other brands from stealing your name, keeps you compliant with federal and state regulations, and ensures your personal assets are safe if things go wrong.

Real-world relevance

In 2019, the streetwear brand BAPE (A Bathing Ape) won a $1 million trademark infringement lawsuit against a smaller brand that used a confusingly similar ape logo. On the flip side, many small brands have lost their names because they didn't file trademarks early enough. The founder of the original 'The Hundreds' brand spent years in legal battles defending their name against copycats. The lesson: trademark protection is not optional — it's essential survival gear.

Key points

  • Choose Your Business Structure — An LLC (Limited Liability Company) is the most popular choice for clothing brands because it protects your personal assets if the business gets sued, offers tax flexibility, and is relatively simple to set up ($50-$500 depending on state). Sole proprietorship is simpler but offers zero personal liability protection.
  • Register Your Business — You need to register with your state, get an EIN (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS (free), and register for state sales tax collection. If selling online to multiple states, you may need to deal with nexus laws — sales tax obligations in states where you have significant sales volume.
  • Trademark Your Brand Name and Logo — A federal trademark (filed through USPTO.gov) costs $250-$350 per class and gives you exclusive nationwide rights to your brand name and logo. Do a trademark search BEFORE choosing your brand name — discovering a conflict after you've printed 5,000 labels is devastating. The process takes 8-12 months.
  • Copyright Protects Your Original Designs — Original textile patterns, graphic prints, and artistic elements of your designs are automatically copyrighted when created. However, copyright does NOT protect clothing designs themselves (the cut, shape, or silhouette) in the US. Fashion design is notoriously under-protected legally, which is why fast fashion can legally copy runway looks.
  • FTC Textile Rules — Labeling Laws — The Federal Trade Commission requires all clothing sold in the US to have labels showing: (1) fiber content by percentage, (2) country of origin, (3) manufacturer/importer identity, and (4) care instructions using standard symbols. Non-compliance can result in fines. This applies even to small brands and handmade sellers.
  • Business Insurance Is Non-Negotiable — At minimum, get general liability insurance ($400-$600/year for small brands). If you have inventory, add product liability insurance. If someone claims your garment caused an allergic reaction, injury, or a child safety issue, insurance is the difference between a bump in the road and bankruptcy.
  • Licenses and Permits — Depending on your location and business type, you may need: a general business license, a home occupation permit (if home-based), a seller's permit for sales tax, and potentially a resale certificate (to buy wholesale without paying sales tax on goods you'll resell). Check your city and state requirements.
  • Contracts With Manufacturers — Never start production without a written agreement covering: pricing, minimum order quantities, quality standards, payment terms, delivery timeline, intellectual property ownership of designs, and what happens if quality is substandard. Verbal agreements in manufacturing lead to heartbreak.
  • Terms of Service and Privacy Policy — Every e-commerce website needs a Terms of Service (your rules for buying) and a Privacy Policy (how you handle customer data). These are legally required in most jurisdictions, especially with GDPR (Europe) and CCPA (California). Templates exist on Shopify and through legal services like Termly.

Code example

=== LEGAL SETUP CHECKLIST ===

BUSINESS FORMATION (Week 1-2)
[ ] Choose business structure (LLC recommended)
[ ] Register with your state ($50-$500)
[ ] Get federal EIN from IRS (free — irs.gov)
[ ] Open a dedicated business bank account
[ ] Set up business accounting (QuickBooks, Wave)
[ ] Check local business license requirements
[ ] Get seller's permit / sales tax registration

INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY (Month 1-3)
[ ] Search USPTO.gov for trademark conflicts
[ ] Search state trademark databases
[ ] Google your desired brand name extensively
[ ] Check domain name availability
[ ] Check social media handle availability
[ ] File federal trademark application ($250-$350)
[ ] Document all original designs with dates

LABELING COMPLIANCE (Before First Sale)
[ ] Fiber content label (e.g., "60% Cotton, 40% Polyester")
[ ] Country of origin label (e.g., "Made in Portugal")
[ ] Manufacturer identification (RN number or company name)
[ ] Care instruction symbols (ASTM D5489 standard)
[ ] Children's clothing: CPSIA compliance if applicable

INSURANCE (Before First Sale)
[ ] General liability insurance ($400-$600/year)
[ ] Product liability insurance ($500-$1,200/year)
[ ] If home-based: check homeowner's policy exclusions
[ ] Consider: commercial property insurance for inventory

E-COMMERCE LEGAL (Before Website Launch)
[ ] Terms of Service
[ ] Privacy Policy (GDPR + CCPA compliant)
[ ] Return/Refund Policy (clear and fair)
[ ] Shipping Policy
[ ] Cookie consent banner

=== CARE LABEL SYMBOLS REFERENCE ===

Symbol     Meaning
-------    ---------------------------------
  W        Wash (number = max temperature °C)
  △        Bleach (X through = do not bleach)
  □        Dry (circle inside = tumble dry)
  ⌂        Iron (dots = heat level 1-3)
  ○        Professional care / dry clean

=== MANUFACTURER CONTRACT ESSENTIALS ===

Must-have clauses:
  1. Pricing per unit and total order cost
  2. MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity)
  3. Production timeline with milestones
  4. Quality standards and inspection rights
  5. Payment terms (typically 30% deposit, 70% on delivery)
  6. IP ownership — YOU own all designs
  7. Confidentiality / NDA clause
  8. Defect policy — what happens with bad units
  9. Shipping terms (FOB, CIF, etc.)
  10. Dispute resolution process

Line-by-line walkthrough

  1. 1. The Legal Setup Checklist is organized chronologically — business formation first, then IP protection, then labeling compliance, then insurance.
  2. 2. Note that trademark searching happens BEFORE filing — discovering a conflict after printing labels and packaging is an expensive mistake.
  3. 3. The labeling compliance section reflects FTC requirements that apply to ALL clothing sellers, regardless of size — even Etsy sellers.
  4. 4. Care label symbols follow international standards, which is important if you ever sell internationally or source from overseas manufacturers.
  5. 5. The manufacturer contract essentials list the 10 clauses that protect you — missing even one (like IP ownership) can lead to a supplier producing your designs for other brands.

Spot the bug

LEGAL SETUP PLAN:
Business structure: Sole proprietorship (saves money!)
Brand name: 'VUITTON VIBES' (sounds luxury)
Trademark: Will file later when we're bigger
Labeling: Just putting our brand name on a tag
Insurance: Can't afford it yet, will skip for now
Contracts: Verbal agreement with manufacturer is fine
Privacy policy: Not needed — we're too small
Need a hint?
Nearly every line in this plan creates a legal risk. How many issues can you spot?
Show answer
Issues: (1) Sole proprietorship means personal liability — if a customer sues, they can take your house and car, (2) 'VUITTON VIBES' would immediately trigger a trademark infringement lawsuit from Louis Vuitton (one of the most litigious brands in fashion), (3) Delaying trademark filing means someone else can claim your name first, (4) Labels missing fiber content, country of origin, and care instructions violates FTC regulations and can result in fines, (5) Skipping insurance is gambling with your financial future — one product liability claim could bankrupt you, (6) Verbal manufacturing agreements offer zero legal protection when disputes arise, (7) Privacy policies are legally required for any website collecting customer data under GDPR and CCPA.

Explain like I'm 5

Imagine you draw an amazing superhero character. You want to put it on t-shirts and sell them. The legal stuff is like calling 'DIBS!' on your superhero (that's a trademark), telling the teacher if someone copies your drawing (that's enforcement), and wearing safety goggles in art class (that's insurance). It's not the fun part, but it keeps bullies from stealing your character and keeps you out of trouble!

Fun fact

The fashion industry loses an estimated $450 billion annually to counterfeiting. Louis Vuitton alone spends over $17 million per year fighting counterfeit products and employs a team of 60 full-time lawyers dedicated solely to intellectual property enforcement. Meanwhile, H&M has been sued multiple times for copying independent designers' prints — and usually settles out of court.

Hands-on challenge

Complete the Legal Setup Checklist for your clothing brand. Even if you're not launching yet, do the research: (1) Look up LLC registration fees in your state, (2) Search USPTO.gov for your desired brand name and report any conflicts, (3) Check domain and social handle availability, (4) Write a sample care label for a product you plan to sell, and (5) Get a general liability insurance quote from an online provider.
